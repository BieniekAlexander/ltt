Albanian[edit]

Etymology 1[edit]

Theoretically from Proto-Albanian *k\xc4\x81kta, of uncertain origin; potentially akin to Lithuanian kokt\xc3\xb9s (\xe2\x80\x9cdisgusting\xe2\x80\x9d) via Proto-Indo-European. An older hypothesis connects it to Ancient Greek \xce\xba\xcf\x8c\xcf\x84\xce\xbf\xcf\x82 (k\xc3\xb3tos, \xe2\x80\x9crancor, grudge\xe2\x80\x9d).[1]

Adverb[edit]

kot

  1. worthlessly
  2. in vain

Adjective[edit]

kot

  1. worthless
  2. pointless

Etymology 2[edit]

From Proto-Albanian *k\xc4\x81ta, from Proto-Indo-European *(s)kHtos (\xe2\x80\x9cshade, shadow, darkness\xe2\x80\x9d). Compare Old Irish sc\xc3\xa1th (\xe2\x80\x9cshadow\xe2\x80\x9d), Ancient Greek \xcf\x83\xce\xba\xcf\x8c\xcf\x84\xce\xbf\xcf\x82 (sk\xc3\xb3tos, \xe2\x80\x9cdarkness\xe2\x80\x9d), Sanskrit \xe0\xa4\x9b\xe0\xa4\xa4\xe0\xa5\x8d\xe0\xa4\xa4\xe0\xa5\x8d\xe0\xa4\xb0 (chattra, \xe2\x80\x9cumbrella\xe2\x80\x9d), English shadow.[2]

Noun[edit]

kot m

  1. darkness

Dutch[edit]

Etymology[edit]

From Middle Dutch cot, cote, from Old Dutch [Term?] cota (in place names), kota (\xe2\x80\x9clittle house\xe2\x80\x9d), from Proto-Germanic *kut\xc4\x85, *kutan (\xe2\x80\x9cshed\xe2\x80\x9d), probably of non-Indo-European origin, but possibly borrowed from Uralic; compare Finnish kota (\xe2\x80\x9chut, house\xe2\x80\x9d) and Hungarian h\xc3\xa1z (\xe2\x80\x9chouse\xe2\x80\x9d), both from Proto-Finno-Ugric/Proto-Uralic *kota. However, compare Dutch and English hut.[1]

Cognate to English cot, cote, German Kate.

Pronunciation[edit]

  • IPA(key): /k\xc9\x94t/
  • audio(file)
  • Hyphenation: kot
  • Rhymes: -\xc9\x94t

Noun[edit]

kot n (plural kotten or koten, diminutive kotje n or kotteke n)

  1. bad, ramshackle housing
    In wat voor een kot woont die! \xe2\x80\x95 What a shack she lives in!
  2. rudimentary building to store (garden) material
  3. (Belgium) student room
    Leuvense koten brandden vaak af in de jaren 90. \xe2\x80\x95 Student rooms in Louvain burned down often in the nineties.
    Synonyms: kamer, studentenkamer, studentenkot

Descendants[edit]

  • Negerhollands: kot, cot
    • \xe2\x86\x92 Virgin Islands Creole: kot (dated)

Related terms[edit]

  • coterie
  • keuter

References[edit]

  1. ^ Kroonen, Guus (2013), \xe2\x80\x9ckuta\xe2\x80\x9d, in Etymological Dictionary of Proto-Germanic (Leiden Indo-European Etymological Dictionary Series; 11), Leiden, Boston: Brill, \xe2\x86\x92ISBN, page 313-14

Polish[edit]

Polish Wikipedia has an article on:

Wikipedia pl

Etymology[edit]

Inherited from Proto-Slavic *kot\xd1\x8a.

Pronunciation[edit]

  • IPA(key): /k\xc9\x94t/
  • Audio(file)
  • Rhymes: -\xc9\x94t
  • Syllabification: kot

Noun[edit]

kot m anim (diminutive kotek or koteczek, augmentative kocur, feminine kocica or kotka)

  1. cat, tomcat
  2. dust bunny
  3. (slang, chiefly military) rookie
  4. (hunting jargon) hare
    • 1834, Adam Mickiewicz, chapter IV, in Pan Tadeusz, lines 934-935, page 189:
      W istocie, kot czu\xc5\x82 s ty\xc5\x82u my\xc5\x9bliwych i psiarnie,
      Rwa\xc5\x82 w pole, s\xc5\x82uchy wytkn\xc4\x85\xc5\x82 jak dwa r\xc3\xb3\xc5\xbcki sarnie
      In very truth, the hare felt behind it the hunters and the pack; it was making for the field; it stretched out behind it its ears like two deer\'s horns [transl. by George Rapall Noyes]
